A Whole Lot of Nothing Going On: Stocks Yawns as Fed Minutes Released, Fighting in Ukraine Continues

  • Email
  • Print
  • Tweet
  • smaller
  • Larger

By Ben Levisohn

A whole lot happened in the world today. You just wouldn’t know it from looking at the U.S. stock market.

Bloomberg News

In actuality, it was really a nothing day. The Dow Jones Industrial Average dipped 0.1% to 18,029.85, while the Nasdaq Composite ticked up 0.1% to 4,906.36, inching ever closer to its dot-com boom highs. The S&P 500 finished little changed at 2,099.68.

You would have thought the market would have moved more, however. From the continued fighting in Ukraine–so much for that ceasefire–to the deadlocked negotiations in Greece to the Federal Reserve, which released the minutes from its January meeting today, there was plenty of market moving news. The market barely budged.

On first glance, the Fed minutes appeared to tilt dovish, but Amherst Pierpont Securities’ Stephen Stanley thinks investors should read the minutes before buying that line of thought:

Newswire headline writers are notorious for creating a misleading picture, but I have rarely seen more distorted headlines from the newswires than the ones that hit the Bloomberg today at 2:00.  If all you read were the two highlighted headlines, these FOMC minutes must seem unbelievably dovish.  In reality, having read the minutes in their entirety, I walk away with the impression that the Committee quite intentionally attempted to publish a set of minutes that would fail to move the needle in either direction with respect to the near-term policy outlook.  If that was indeed their intention, they were blown out of the water by a zealous headline writer.

Ameriprise Financial’s David Joy ponders the Nasdaq’s approach to its record high (not adjusting for inflation or dividends of course):

[The] NASDAQ Composite index is closing in on a new high of its own, only for the first time in 15 years. At the height of the dot-com bubble in 2000, the NASDAQ peaked at a level of 5,048 on March 10, before plunging 78 percent to a low of 1,114 in October 2002…

Apple (AAPL) is getting a lot of the credit as the largest component in the index and whose share price is higher by 16 percent so far this year and 64 percent over the past twelve months. That likely matters little to those who own the index, who will take performance wherever they can get it.

If and when the NASDAQ does establish a new high, earnings will be the primary differentiator from the last time. When the internet first burst onto the scene, investors were at a loss over how to properly value these new companies. The debate boiled down to the question of whether earnings or eyeballs were the most important metric, and Wall Street guessed wrong. What it overlooked was the ability to monetize those website visits, and turn them into profit. In the process, the price-to-earnings (P/E) multiple of the NASDAQ climbed near 200x. No one wanted to be left behind.

Today, the P/E multiple is a much more fundamentally sound 30x on a trailing earnings basis and 20x looking ahead twelve months. Rounding out the top five largest companies in the index are household name including Microsoft (MSFT), Google (GOOG), Intel (INTC), and Facebook (FB). So, we should welcome a new high in the NASDAQ, as it will indicate that it, and we, have matured in our understanding of the internet and its possibilities.

Who says you can never go home again?

    Until last year, few investors doubted the benefits of holding one or more integrated oil stocks in a portfolio. But then the price of oil fell in half in just a few months, and even these normally stable performers began to fall sharply as well.

    Some value investors think that now is not the time to be selling Big Oil. They argue that these stocks, which sport among the best dividend yields in the large-cap space, look cheap and could perform nicely if oil continues a price turnaround that began in late January.

    But in a fresh piece on the CNNMoney site, writer Matt Egan wonders “did Buffett, who has made a career out of being a long-term investor, sell at the bottom?”

    CNNMoney

    Time to Buy Big-Oil Stocks?

    Egan refers to a recent report by BlackRock, the asset management firm, which contends that integrated oil and gas companies are its favorite subsector right now in the beaten-down energy sector. BlackRock favors the “super majors” because of their strong balance sheets, high dividends and integrated business models. These stocks are also sporting forward price-to-earnings multiples that trail both the broader stock market and the subsector’s history.

    Indeed, Exxon is up 6% from its recent lows.

    While oil services firms still look risky to BlackRock, it likes that integrated oil companies are sporting a 12-month forward price-to-earnings of 10 times, which is below their historical levels and global equities broadly.

    Still, even Andrew Bary, a veteran Barron’s writer, isn’t convinced that now is the time to buy Big Oil. In a Barron’s piece last weekend, Bary urges investors to avoid integrated companies, including Exxon, Chevron and Conoco.

    He argues that there’s little upside to their shares even if oil rallies.

    Meanwhile, Morningstar, in a periodic piece on the investment moves and stock selections of its highest-rated fund managers, discloses the top 10 “high conviction” purchases of its better managers.

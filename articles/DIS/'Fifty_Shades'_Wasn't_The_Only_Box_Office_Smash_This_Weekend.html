    'Fifty Shades' Wasn't The Only Box Office Smash This Weekend

    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    Fifty Shades of Grey dominated the weekend box office over the Presidents Day holiday, but would-be runner up Kingsman: The Secret Service was a bit hit in its own right

    Most of the weekend’s box office punditry this weekend was focused on Fifty Shades of Grey, and for good reason. The $40 million R-rated erotic drama from Universal/Comcast Corp. explicitly made by and for woman earned a superb $267 million worldwide over its first five days of international play, including $94 million over its Friday-to-Monday domestic debut. But it was not the only big release this weekend, and the other major release this weekend was not only a big hit in-and-of-itself but also somewhat of an unconventional would-be blockbuster as well. I am speaking of course of 20th Century Fox’s Kingsman: The Secret Service. While the Dakota Johnson/Jamie Dornan vehicle Fifty Shades of Grey was busy pulling down $94 million over President’s Day Weekend, Kingsman: The Secret Service grossed a strong $42 million over its four-day domestic debut. Writer/director Matthew Vaughn and co-writer Jane Goldman’s action comedy may not have had the oodles of free press that Fifty Shades of Grey received in the run-up to its release, but it too was a hard-R genre entry in a sea of PG-13 spectacles, and yet its success occurred somewhat under the radar. Why did I start humming the theme song to Tomorrow Never Dies?

    It was based not on an international bestseller but on a cult comic book written by Mark Millar of Kick-Ass and Wanted fame. The film is a delightfully daffy and mostly successful homage to the kind of James Bond films best represented by the Roger Moore-era adventures. It is also a film that was heavily pitched to the geek crowd to such an extent that I was a little worried that the film would end up falling victim to the Kick-Ass Curse. By that I mean Matthew Vaughn’s 2010 adaptation of the superhero deconstruction that played great for the geeks but was somewhat ignored by general audiences. Since Kick-Ass did earned $98m worldwide in theaters and play well on DVD/Blu-Ray, better examples might be Scott Pilgrim vs. the World and Dredd. Both of which were absolutely beloved by the Comic Con crowd (and extensively sold to said crowd) but were utterly ignored by the masses when their theatrical debuts rolled around. The Edgar Wright video game-inspired romantic fantasy earned just $47m worldwide on a $60m budget for Universal while the second Dredd film earned just $35m worldwide on a $50m budget for Lions Gate Entertainment.

    I did have initial concerns that the brutally violent and geek-friendly spy comedy would face similar obstacles, especially as leading man Colin Firth was not exactly an opener and Samuel L. Jackson is the mother of all added value elements but hasn’t been a by-himself opener since (the genuinely underrated) Lakeview Terrace back in 2008. But I was wrong. Maybe I was wrong to worry in the first place, as Wanted opened with $50 million in June of 2008 against the $63m debut of Walt Disney/Pixar’s Wall-E. Of course Wanted featured an “in her prime as an action heroine” Angelina Jolie and an amusingly against-type Morgan Freeman, along with a trailer-friendly “curved bullet” gimmick. But Fox did right by the picture, getting the geek demographic on board early with a premiere at Harry Knowles’s “Butt-Numb-A-Thon” birthday celebration in December of last year. But before that they had the good fortune to have a stupidly successful 2014, which allowed the various Kingsman trailers to play in front of hits like X-Men” Days of Future Past, Dawn of the Planet of the Apes, Let’s Be Cops, and Gone Girl along with Taken 3 in early 2015.

    I hate that the trailers shamefully gave away major second and third act story beats. I consider myself fortunate that I didn’t see enough of the marketing prior to my early screening to be spoiled in terms of a few jaw-dropping turns. But as much as I hated the “lay the entire story out in the trailers” method and as much as I loathed the spoiler-filled Super Bowl trailer, I will admit that it was perhaps a necessary evil in order to sell a film that, to the vast majority of moviegoers, was an original property. By the way, Fox could have burned money on a truncated version of the Fantastic Four teaser, but instead they dropped the $4.5 million on a last-ditch Kingsman sell instead, showing off the film to a deluge of action-hungry would-be moviegoers. And that Fantastic Four teaser was a brilliant (if obvious) play, dropping in time for the film’s UK debut and inspiring hundreds of blog posts all over the Internet discussing the trailer in detail while also plugging Kingsman at the same time in the weeks before its US release. The end result is that the $81m Kingsman: The Secret Service scored a terrific $42m over the Fri-Mon President’s Day weekend, good for the seventh-biggest debut on said weekend ever.

    The picture has earned $85.8m thus far worldwide. Give or take competition from male-targeted action releases like Sony’s Chappie, Sean Penn’s The Gunman, and Liam Neeson’s Run All Night (Warner Bros./Time Warner Inc.) between now and the April 3rd debut of Furious 7, the crowd pleasing Kingsman: The Secret Service has a shot at a somewhat leggy run under the shadow of Fifty Shades of Grey. It is a little fitting that the film, which is a textbook male escapist fantasy, did so well on the same weekend as a towering example of the female escapist fantasy. Moreover, there is a history of runner-ups (Tomorrow Never Dies, Mamma Mia!, Sherlock Holmes, etc.) doing just fine in the shadow of box office monsters (Titanic, The Dark Knight, Avatar) and that I’m sure Fox doesn’t care less which film came in number #1 this weekend. To paraphrase the film’s catchphrase, rank not maketh hit. Grosses maketh hit, and Kingsman: The Secret Service is potentially on-its-way to become both a hit and a new franchise. Matthew Vaughn, Jane Goldman, and the marketing department at 20th Century Fox scored a big winner this weekend from a not-quite-sure thing. I thought I ought to tell you about it.

    Report: Disney to hike Magic Kingdom ticket above $100

    By Kevin Chupka 43 minutes ago
    • 
    • 
    • 
    • 
    Content preferences

      The Tampa Bay Times and other sources in central and southern Florida are reporting that this year's seeimingly-annual price hike at Walt Disney World (DIS) will make a one day ticket to the Magic Kingdom eclipse $100 for the first time.

      The price of admission at Walt Disney World's Magic Kingdom is about to eclipse $100 a day

      Under the plan that “Disney insiders” seems to have leaked to certain members of the press, a ticket to the Magic Kingdom will go from $99 to $105. The other three Florida Parks (Epcot, Hollywood Studios and Animal Kingdom) will go from $94 to $99.

      Get the Latest Market Data and News with the Yahoo Finance App

      Will visitors lash out at the price hike? Unlikely says Yahoo Finance’s Jeff Macke. “Much like Las Vegas you’re gonna leave with your wallet entirely empty,” he says.

      Macke notes that Disney’s most recent earnings report, one that coincidently sent the stock over $100 for the first time, showed strong numbers for the company’s American parks. In short, they can afford to risk a higher price in Florida.

      Save a few more pennies if you want to see Mickey!

      But Macke doesn’t think there’s much risk there anyway. “It’s a better park experience if you kind of limit the access by increasing the expense of going there… It’s a big headline number and people can pretend to be outraged but they’re still gonna go.”

      Mike Santoli notes that a better park experience is something Disney has been working on for a while. New attractions and technologies will keep customers coming back for more.

      Related: Disney earnings: How long can Frozen keep making money?

      One key amenity was the introduction of the Magic Band in 2013. The fitness wristband look-a-like allows guests to get into their hotel room, pay for meals and use the popular FastPass feature in the parks.

      “You’re getting more out of the day at the park, in theory, from those bands,” Santoli argues adding a price hike at the Magic Kingdom isn’t much of a problem because “stuff that people want to do is always the stuff that is going to outpace inflation in price.”

      If nothing else Disney has proven a visit to their parks falls squarely into the category of “stuff people want to do.”

    'Fifty Shades Of Grey' Dominates Box Office With Record $94.4M Weekend, $266M Global Bow

    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    Fifty Shades of Grey began its box box office domination with a (a)rousing $85 million Fri-Sun debut over the President’s Day weekend, including $8.6m worth of Thursday previews and a $30m opening day. That means that the film earned a 2.83x weekend multiplier, which is pretty huge for a blockbuster with the caveat that it opened over a holiday weekend. Its $94.4m Fri-Mon haul indeed easily bests the $62m Fri-Mon debut of Valentine’s Day for the biggest President’s Day weekend haul in history. Among R-rated Fri-Sun debuts, it’s the fourth-biggest debut behind The Hangover part II ($85.9m), American Sniper ($89.2m), and The Matrix Reloaded ($91.7m). It is the biggest opening weekend ever for a film directed by a woman*, topping the $69.6m debut of Catherine Hardwicke’s Twilight, the $67.3m debut of Frozen, and the $66.3m debut of Brave. This is a smashing start, the biggest debut weekend in February history, to a most unusual would-be blockbuster.

    Fifty Shades of Grey is an R-rated would-be franchise starter featuring a female as its lead (Dakota Johnson) and explicitly targeting adult women as its prime demographic. It’s directed by a woman (Sam Taylor-Johnson), written by a woman (Kelly Marcel), and based on a book written by a woman (E.L. James). Any one of those details would make it abnormal it today’s landscape. All of them combined are almost a statistical impossibility. So yes, regardless of whether you like the film and regardless of whether you approve of its content, its unqualified box office victory is a huge deal and a net-positive for those like me who whine about gender parity in Hollywood. It is of course its adapted from E.L. James’s book which began life as Twilight fan-fiction, but its success may-well mean more female-centric romantic/erotic dramas and may mean more women getting the chance to direct mainstream studio features. It also (god willing) finally crushes the notion that female-centric films cannot achieve blockbuster status and most certainly crushes the notion that female-centric films need male ticket buyers to play at the grown ups’ table. Love the movie or hate it, but this performance is a huge win for female moviegoers and female moviemakers.

    Universal/Comcast Comcast Corp. did a heck of a job turning this one into a mainstream event. There were newsworthy trailer drops (the first during Good Morning America back in July, the second during an episode of Scandal, and a high-profile Super Bowl spot as well) and an unending stream of stories discussing the various controversies (who the fans wanted to be cast, Charlie Hunnam taking the role of Mr. Grey and then backing out, whether or not the film would be as explicit as the book, etc.) which kept the film in the news cycle for the last seven months. The last couple weeks had would-be news detailing the alleged lack of chemistry between the stars on the worldwide press circuit (shocker: they are not in love and were not into the film’s BDSM sequences) as well as alleged artistic squabbles between the director and the author (which sadly will possibly lead to Ms. Taylor-Johnson not returning for the sequel). All of which led to the actual reviews which turned out to be a bit better than expected. Not even the Marvel/Spider-Man news could keep this one out of the limelight. The film played 58% 25 years old and up, 68% female, 52% Caucasian, 22% Hispanic, 15% African American, 7% Asian, and 4% other.

    The end result is something of a true event film aimed at the audiences that almost never get true event films: older women. The film scored huge overseas as well, earning an estimated $172m outside of America since opening on Thursday (easily crushing the $117m overseas debut of The Matrix Reloaded to become the top R-rated international debut), which gives the $40m drama a whopping $266.4m worldwide as of Monday, give or take how it does on Monday. In America, pre-sales were heavy in Southern “Bible Belt” states, which again goes to show the value of making movies not necessarily aimed at nerds and the LA/NYC crowd. Like American Sniper, Fifty Shades of Grey redefines the kind of film that can be a blockbuster, and if that lesson gets remembered alongside the obvious box office dominations of Avengers: Age of Ultron and Star Wars: The Force Awakens, then 2015 will be a game-changer for the industry overall.

    For what it’s worth, and we’re only dealing with one weekend of box office at the moment, I may have been wrong to complain awhile back about removing the film’s more explicit content for the sake of general audiences, and it’s possible that reassurances that it wouldn’t be too kinky, as well as the aforementioned not-that-bad reviews may have or will eventually convince the casually curious to see what all of the fuss is all about. If the film doesn’t sink like a stone after this weekend, an a CinemaScore grade of C+ leaves that somewhat in doubt as does the front loaded nature of the weekend overall, I will offer a mea culpa accordingly. I still think the film would have done just fine without the toning down of said material and/or more shots of Jamie Dornan’s penis. And yes I admit that we probably wouldn’t be talking about a $81.6 million opening weekend if Universal had been courageous enough to go for an NC-17 and release the film in as many theaters as they could to test the waters, but how I wish they had tried anyway.

    Okay, the rest of the box office news, including the rock-solid performance of Kingsman: The Secret Service and the hold for The SpongeBob Movie: Sponge Out of Water, is HERE. And join me next week for the debut weekend of Walt Disney's Walt Disney's McFarland, USA, Paramount (Viacom Viacom Inc.’) Hot Tub Time Machine 2, and Lions Gate Entertainment’s The Duff.

    * I didn’t catch make the connection at the time, but for the person formerly known as Larry Wachowski (sibling of Andy Wachowski) underwent a gender transition some years after The Matrix Reloaded opened and is now Lana Wachowski, so perhaps The Matrix Reloaded technically is the biggest debut weekend by a female director.

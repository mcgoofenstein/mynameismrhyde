    'Interstellar' Returns To IMAX For One Night Only, Because 'Why Not?'

    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    AMC Entertainment and IMAX Corporation announced yesterday that Chris Nolan’s Interstellar will be kinda-sorta returning to theaters. The Paramount/Viacom Inc. release will play for (to quote the Dreamgirls song) “One Night Only” in thirty IMAX theaters around the country. The screenings will take place on Saturday, February 21st, at 3:00pm. As a bonus of sorts, audiences who attend the “encore presentation” will get twelve minutes of behind-the-scenes footage. What is interesting about the presentation is that there doesn’t seem to be any ulterior motivation behind it. At the risk of sounding glib, IMAX, Paramount, and AMC are returning Interstellar to theaters “just because.” And frankly, in terms of giving audiences a chance to see a film like Interstellar on a high quality theater one last time, I’d argue “just because” is good enough.

    I would love to say that the screening is about swaying the Academy voters in advance of the Oscars, but voting will be done long before the “day before the show” screening. The picture is nominated for five Academy Awards, and it is arguably the frontrunner to win Best Visual Effects and (ironically) Best Sound Editing and Best Sound unless American Sniper rides its current wave to the respective prizes (as a somewhat general rule, the films nominated in the top-tier categories tend to beat the ones that are nominated only in the technical categories). I wouldn’t mind seeing Hans Zimmer win for his score either as, my issues with mixing aside, it’s a powerful piece of film composing and I have used it as “Stop checking Facebook and Twitter and just get this review done!” background music several times over the last few months. And I would love to say that it’s an advertisement of sorts for the film’s upcoming home video release, but the film doesn’t drop on DVD and Blu Ray until March 31st. Even the film’s VOD debut isn’t until March 17th, after which you can watch the film the way it was meant to be seen, on your smartphone while you ride to-and-from work on public transportation.

    For the record, the Blu Ray is boasting a solid set of features so kudos to Paramount for not skimping on that department. In terms of Interstellar, this doesn’t really seem to have any value. The $165 million Matthew McConaughey/Anne Hathaway/Jessica Chastain/Michael Caine sci-fi drama has basically ended its theatrical run with $187 million domestic and $484m overseas for a $671m worldwide cume on a $165m budget. By the way, the overseas distribution was handled by Warner Bros./Time Warner Inc. in exchange for giving Paramount back the rights to Friday the 13th and South Park among other things. So let’s hope that the next Friday the 13th reboot earns $484m worldwide to make up the difference. Jokes aside, this was a fine performance a wholly original science fiction drama, especially considering that the premise and respective leading man (Matthew McConaughey versus Leonardo DiCaprio) were inherently less commercial than Inception. I have my issues with the film, but I can only hope they will mellow over time and I still consider its leggy success an overall victory for the kinds of films we want to see in our multiplexes.

    I might argue that this release is merely about attempting to manufacture interest in the respective IMAX theaters during what has turned into a somewhat slow period, but again we’re only talking about one screening in thirty screens. American Sniper turned out to be a last-minute boon for the month of January and we’ve seen a number of outside-the-box choices over the last couple months, from the Game of Thrones event to the upcoming Will Smith drama Focus. Toss in the March 6th IMAX release of Sony’s Chappie and the March 13th debut of Walt Disney’s Cinderella and you have a situation where IMAX is starting to become less like a “we take one big film and run it for a while” venue and more like a “if you see it in the first week you can go for the IMAX Experience.” Now of course there will be films that get extended runs (like, for example Interstellar) but we’re starting to see a clearer delineation of films that are extended IMAX runs and films that are, proverbially speaking, for one night only (sorry, Effie, but Deena Jones’s disco version is better).

    The end result is you have situations like Fifty Shades of Grey, which was converted to IMAX in a couple days on 75 screens and may-or-may not expand next week to minimize the bloodshed of Seventh Son and Jupiter Ascending. By the way, Fifty Shades of Grey (Universal/Comcast Corp.) played on 320 PLF screens and earned $8.2m (9%) over the four day debut. Or you have circumstances where Tom Cruise all-but-admitted on Friday that Paramount moved Mission: Impossible 5 from December 23rd, 2015 to July 31st, 2015 mostly to get available IMAX screens that otherwise were being monopolized by J.J. “I only shot one scene in IMAX” Abrams’s Star Wars: The Force Awakens. Anyway, tangentially related digressions aside, I am pleased that Paramount has once again found a way to turn a would-be premium theatrical engagement into an event. They tried all manner of gimmicks with Interstellar, from the 48 hours-early “film only” release to the “see Interstellar in theaters as many times as you want for one low price” gimmick. I’m not convinced either of these ideas boosted the overall box office (Do you know anyone who bought the “pay for a day, play all year” Interstellar pass?), but damned if Paramount and related interested parties didn’t stress the importance of seeing Interstellar in theaters.

    Now I hope they don’t do something like dropping a Batman v Superman: Dawn of Justice trailer or a Mission: Impossible 5 teaser before the show, as I won’t be anywhere near a computer on Saturday afternoon. So, if you’re reading this Paramount, Warner Bros., and IMAX, please don’t do that…

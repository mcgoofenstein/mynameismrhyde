    Review: 'The Second Best Exotic Marigold Hotel' Is The Second Best Film In The Series

    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    Thumbnail: The film is hobbled by the terrible character arc for its young lead, but it gets by on the sheer charm and chemistry of its elders.

    Box Office:
    If ever there was a film that didn’t seem on paper like the kind of film that would inspire a sequel, it is The Best Exotic Marigold Hotel. The John Madden picture, released courtesy of Fox Searchlight (20th Century Fox) concerned a group disconnected senior citizens that travel to India for varied  reasons and end up helping a young man (Dev Patel) renovate and take over a crumbling hotel. The film, based on Deborah Moggach’s 2004 novel These Foolish Things, was basically a British export, produced by Participant Media and Blueprint Pictures on a budget of just $10 million and ended up grossing $136m worldwide, including a leggy domestic run that earned $46m despite opening on the same day as The Avengers.

    Thus three years later, John Madden, writer Ol Parker, and the original cast (including Dev Patel, Judi Dench, Bill Nighy, and Maggie Smith) have returned with new cast members Richard Gere and David Strathairn for The Second Best Exotic Marigold Hotel. Unlike the Walt Disney/Marvel films, this franchise did not magically revive the key character who died in the last film. This one opens in the UK on February 27th, 2015 and in America on March 6th in limited release. If this film is a hit anywhere near the level of the predecessor, I’m sure we will get a Third Best Exotic Marigold Hotel in a few years.

    The Review:
     It can be argued that the audience for a film like The Second Best Exotic Marigold Hotel don’t necessarily flock to the theater based on tight plot or even original narratives. That’s not a criticism, as you could say that about any number of films in any number of genres. But to the extent that the first film has a fanbase and to the extent that this film will find its audience, it will be on the draw of both the Indian locations and the strong cast of “too old to be a superhero” cast. I bring this up because the sequel is a film that works very much despite itself. The film somewhat struggles to justify itself in terms of a new story or new arcs for its characters, it does get by on the sheer charisma of its cast. I often talk about films being “an acting treat,” and this one certainly qualifies.     To wit, you put Judi Dench, Bill Nighy, Maggie Smith, Richard Gere, David Strathairn, Ronald Pickup, and Lillete Dubey in the same movie and you’re probably going to get a film worth seeing. It’s a pity that the main story arc does so much to self-sabotage itself that the film only barely gets a recommendation.

    The arc I speak of is one offered to Dev Patel. This is not related to Mr. Patel’s natural charisma or skills as an actor, as as Patel is the guy who kept his dignity in M. Night Shyamalan’s The Last Airbender. But his story, which requires him to say absolutely the wrong thing, react in exactly the wrong way, and behave in precisely the most inappropriate manner for 90% of the running time very nearly sinks the film. Sonny Kapoor is now the co-manager alongside Muriel Donnelly (Maggie Smith), and his dreams of expansion along with his stress in planning his wedding bring out the worst in him, from professional incompetence to scathingly obnoxious jealousy. Unfortunately Mr. Patel is forced to start at 100 from the opening scene, leaving no room to build and thus spending the first 3/4 of the film as a cringe-worthy schmuck before he arbitrarily comes to his senses. The only reason this doesn’t sink the film is because Mr. Patel’s attempts to juggle personal and professional responsibilities are not why anyone wants to see this film.

    And the good news is that the various senior citizens of the cast get plenty to do and “steal” the picture. Judi Dench has strong chemistry with Bill Nighy and Maggie Smith struggles with unpleasant news, news which is all the more poignant in never being uttered aloud. Richard Gere pops up as an author or may or may not be a hotel inspector while David Strathairn shows up in an extended cameo and shows that his middle name should really be “world-weary gravitas.” Most of the arcs involve romantic entanglements, the couples established at the end of the first film not adjusted to not falling in love but staying in love, although obviously there aren’t a ton of major studio releases dealing with romance in the later years of life. The film lacks the sense of discovery of the first film and the surprise factor of seeing all of these famous faces in one character-driven comedy. There is a feeling of running in place or re-learning the same lessons, as is often the case when a sequel comes about purely because of an unexpected financial success.

    Ben Smithard’s cinematography is gorgeous and the film goes out of its way to avoid treating the indigenous Indian people like “exotic” scenery in their own country. There are a few subplots that actually center around the locals, and Sonny’s mother (Lillete Dubey) gets a sweet quasi-romance with Richard Gere. It is great fun to see these actors playing together in this (mostly) light-as-a-feather concoction. Still, the film lacks Tom Wilkinson’s somber presence, and the core storyline involving Sonny’s attempts to impress Richard Gere’s would-be inspector, purchase a second hotel, and fend off his own jealousy over his fiancee’s handsome friend brings the picture to a screeching halt every time. Again, this is not Patel’s fault, but sadly the filmmakers couldn’t think of anything to do with the newly successful hotel owner other than to basically turn him into an incompetent idiot for most of the film.

    The Second Best Exotic Hotel nonetheless barely skates by on the strength of its older cast members, which of course is “the reason for the season” and thus counts for much in terms of negating the somewhat lazy storytelling. The film ends on a high note of earned emotion and pathos, and Penelope Wilton has a surprisingly moving third-act cameo. I cannot imagine anyone who truly loved the first film not enjoying the opportunity to revisit this world, and Maggie Smith and Judi Dench are both superb throughout. It is the second best film in the franchise out of two possible films, but in this case second-best is still barely just good enough.

      Summary:
      IBM reported fourth-quarter 2014 results with earnings beating the Zacks Consensus Estimate but declining year over year. Fourth-quarter revenues not only missed the consensus mark but also declined year over year. However, we believe this may not be a bad thing since it is currently transitioning to higher-growth markets that are not yielding enough to offset declines in traditional segments. IBM's investments in cloud computing, Big Data, mobile and security, and the divestitures of its loss-making units ensure long-term profitability. But transition-related uncertainties and intensifying competition remain concerns right now. Thus, we have a Neutral recommendation on IBM shares and set a price target of $166.00.

      Overview:

      International Business Machines Corporation (IBM) was incorporated in the State of New York in 1911. The company is engaged in the development and manufacturing of advanced information technology, including computer systems, software, storage systems and microelectronics.

      IBM reported revenues of $92.8 billion in 2014. Its major operations are categorized into the Global Services segment, Software segment, Systems and Technology segment and Global Financing segment.

      Revenue Details as per Business Segments and Geography:

      The Global Services segment includes IBM's own software and hardware, as well as those provided by other suppliers. Approximately 60.0% of external Global Services revenues is annuity based, coming primarily from outsourcing and maintenance. Under Global Services there are two reportable segments: Global Technology Services GTS and Global Business Services GBS. The GTS segment primarily includes IT infrastructure services and business process services. The GBS segment mainly comprises consulting and application management services.

      The Software segment consists primarily of middleware and operating systems software. IBM middleware is designed for open standards, which allows the integration of disparate client applications that may have been built internally, or provided by package software vendors or system integrators. Approximately two-thirds of external software segment revenues is annuity-based, coming from recurring license charges and on-going subscription and support from one-time charge (OTC) arrangements. The remaining one-third of external revenues relate to OTC arrangements, in which the client pays an upfront payment for a perpetual license.

      The company's software portfolio includes WebSphere software, Information Management software, Tivoli software, Watson solutions, Rational software, Social workforce solutions and Mobile software.

      The Systems and Technology segment provides clients with business solutions requiring advanced computing power and storage capabilities. While 50% of the Systems and Technology segment's server and storage sales transactions are carried out through the company's business partners, the remaining 50% are sold directly to end-user clients. In addition, Systems and Technology provides semiconductor technology and products, packaging solutions and engineering technology services to clients.

      The systems portfolio includes System z, Power Systems and System x that primarily cater to data centers. These systems form the base for integrated offerings, such as IBM PureSystems, IBM Smart Analytics, IBM Pure Data System for Analytics powered by Netezza, IBM Smart Cloud Entry and IBM Blade Center for Cloud. All IBM servers run on Linux.

      Global Financing includes the financing of assets, management of associated risks and reduced debt leverage. The segment comprises three lines of business: Client financing, Commercial financing and Remarketing.

      The company operates in 175 countries. IBM faces significant competition in most of the segments it operates. The main competitors include Oracle, Dell, Hewlett-Packard, EMC and NetApp.

    Yes, IBM Can Adapt To The As-A-Service Economy; Here's Why

    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    The post-recessionary era has been defined by technological changes and the resultant shifts in customer behavior, competitive landscape and profit margins have severely affected legacy IT vendors like IBM. My last column focused on the resulting damage inflicted on Big Blue and others, but claimed the financial and organizational pain has a silver lining for customers in the form of better products and services at lower prices: Survival of the Fittest as applied to business. It might have appeared I was writing IBM’s obituary, however I don’t believe the company, or competitors like HP, Dell, SAP and others that have feasted off fat corporate IT budgets and overworked staffers for decades, are in, or necessarily approaching an irreversible death spiral. However these old tech standbys must adapt to a significantly different business environment, one fostered by the nexus of several factors:

    • rapidly commodified technology
    • universal network connectivity and globalized markets
    • significantly greater IT economies of scale promoting larger consolidation of infrastructures
    • enormous data generation and accumulation resulting in deeper data analysis and business insights
    • a consumer preference for services over products and subscriptions over purchases

    Collectively these have created what some call the As-a-Service Economy. Indeed, the growth of services as a share of total economic consumption is a trend going back decades.

    Adapting to this new economic environment means IBM must transition from primarily selling proprietary systems and complex, customized IT services to purveying standardized, modular, usage based services that are easily integrable with third-party products. From a focus on consulting, design and implementation of one-off IT systems, IBM must rechannel its technical prowess towards building XaaS products and integration services for the as-a-service-economy.

    Recent data shows a sharp increase in relative spending on IT infrastructure services versus manufactured products. Although IBM has capitalized on this shift, it now faces a business climate in the proliferation of cloud services, modular, extensible software with public APIs and business strategies favoring rapidly adjustable operating expenses instead of fixed capital outlays, that is undermining its lucrative services franchise.

    IBM does the vast majority of its business, almost 60%, selling technology and business services. Hardware systems amount to a mere 10% of total revenues, a share set to shrink further since the former System X revenue will be gone for the entire fiscal year. So the company is already committed to the services business, just not the type of usage-based, pay-as-you-go subscriptions customers increasingly prefer: the type that improve business agility, efficiency, capital utilization and hence profitability.

    Source: http://www.ibm.com/investor/att/pdf/IBM-4Q14-Earnings-Charts.pdf

    The litany of major XaaS success stories is well known and their success impressive:

    • Salesforce.com, which has grown revenue at a CAGR of almost 38% over the last decade to over $5 billion
    • Amazon, which has single-handled created the IaaS business in AWS, and arguably leads the industry in innovation, mindshare and maybe even revenue (we’ll soon find out for certain once Amazon starts breaking out its cloud business in earnings reports)
    • Even Microsoft, where customers flocked to Office 365, Azure and Dynamics CRM Online driving commercial cloud revenue up 114% last quarter to an annualized rate of $5.5 billion.
